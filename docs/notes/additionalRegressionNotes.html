Model selection

Setup:

\[ y_i = z_1 \beta_1 x_{i, 1} + \cdots + z_p \beta_p x_{i, p} + \epsilon_i \]

  • \(y_i \in \mathbb{R}\)
  • \(\beta_i \in \mathbb{R}\)
  • \(z_i \in \{0, 1\}\)

The \(z_j\)’s indicate which regression coefficients are non-zero.

Note

Notice that every set of values \(\mathbf{z} = \{z_1, \ldots z_p \}\) corresponds to a different model. So a prior \(p(z)\) may be thought of as a prior distribution over models.

Bayesian model selection proceeds by obtaining a posterior distribution for \(\mathbf{z}\):

\[ p(\mathbf{z}| \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z})}{\sum_\mathbf{\tilde{z}} p(\mathbf{\tilde{z}}) p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{\tilde{z}})} \]

Alternatively, we may prefer to compare any two models with the posterior odds:

\[ \text{odds}(\mathbf{z}_a, \mathbf{z}_b | \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{z}_a | \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X})}{p(\mathbf{z}_b | \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X})} = \frac{p(\mathbf{z}_a)}{p(\mathbf{z}_b)} \times \frac{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z}_a)}{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z}_b)} \]

Notice that when examining the odds of one model vs another, we avoid computing the denominator of \(p(\mathbf{z}| \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X})\), and thereby exhaustively computing the probability of every model.

Definitions
  • \(\frac{p(\mathbf{z}_a)}{p(\mathbf{z}_b)}\) is called the “prior odds”.
  • \(\frac{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z}_a)}{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z}_b)}\) is called the “Bayes factor” i.e. how much the data favor model \(\mathbf{z}_a\) over model \(\mathbf{z}_b\).

This formulation above elicits a need to compute \(p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z})\). To compute this in closed form, we will choose a few special priors (given a MVN data generative process).

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{y}| \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{z}, \sigma^2 &\sim MVN(X \text{diag}(\mathbf{z})\beta, \sigma^2 I)\\ \beta_z | \mathbf{X}_z, \sigma^2 &\sim MVN(\boldsymbol{0}, g\sigma^2 [\mathbf{X}_z^T \mathbf{X}_z]^{-1})\\ 1/\sigma^2 &\sim \text{gamma}(\nu_0/2, \nu_0 \sigma_0^2 /2) \end{aligned} \]
